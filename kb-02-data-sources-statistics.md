## AI adoption and readiness

**AI pilot failure rate**
- Claim: 85% of enterprise AI pilots never reach production
- Source: McKinsey Global Survey on AI, 2023/2024 composite
- Caveat: The exact percentage varies by survey year and methodology. Use "more than 8 in 10" as a conservative alternative if challenged. Always cite McKinsey.
- Slide usage: Full-bleed stat callout on problem slides

**AI investment vs. return gap**
- Claim: Most organizations report AI investments underperforming expectations in year one
- Source: Gartner AI Hype Cycle and annual CIO survey (multiple years)
- Caveat: Gartner updates this annually — use "Gartner research" as attribution, not a specific year percentage, unless Will has a specific report pulled
- Slide usage: Background claim in problem framing, not a headline stat

**AI adoption blockers**
- Claim: Data quality, talent gaps, and governance absence are the top three cited blockers to AI production deployment
- Source: IBM Institute for Business Value, AI in Business report; McKinsey composite
- Slide usage: Supports the five-domain diagnostic framing

**Generative AI productivity claims (handle with caution)**
- Various vendors and consultancies cite 20–40% productivity improvements
- These are self-reported or vendor-sponsored — always attribute to the specific sponsor
- Never present vendor-sponsored productivity claims as independent research on an executive slide
- If challenged by a CFO or CIO: "These are vendor-reported figures. Our engagement will baseline your specific workflows before projecting outcomes."

## Documentation debt and knowledge management

**Cost of failed information searches**
- Claim: $28,000 per knowledge worker annually lost to failed information searches
- Source: IDC, "The Hidden Costs of Information Work" — widely cited, older study (2012) that has been updated in spirit by subsequent IDC research
- Caveat: This is the most-cited figure in knowledge management discussions but is dated. Use with "IDC research estimates" framing, not as a current-year precise figure. If a CFO presses: "This is an IDC benchmark composite. We can baseline your specific cost with a time-audit in week one of the engagement."
- Slide usage: Large stat callout on cost slides

**Onboarding documentation impact**
- Claim: Poor onboarding documentation adds 4–6 weeks to time-to-productivity for new hires
- Source: SHRM onboarding research composite; corroborated by multiple HR analytics vendors
- Caveat: Ranges vary widely by role complexity. Use "4–6 weeks" as a conservative enterprise technology estimate
- Slide usage: Proof point in business case and ROI slides

**Support ticket volume traced to documentation gaps**
- Claim: 30–40% of tier-1 support tickets trace back to missing or incorrect documentation
- Source: Composite from Forrester, HDI (Help Desk Institute), and Gartner service desk research
- Caveat: This range is widely cited but methodologies differ. Frame as "industry research suggests" on the slide. If challenged: "We validate this in your environment during the audit."
- Slide usage: Cost breakdown slides, business case evidence

**Documentation debt and AI training data**
- Claim: Undocumented or inconsistently documented processes cannot be used as AI training or grounding data
- Source: This is a first-principles technical claim, not a research statistic — no external citation needed
- Slide usage: Can be stated as fact in AI readiness diagnostic decks

**AI pilot failure linked to poor data/documentation**
- Claim: 40% of AI pilots are blocked by undocumented or inconsistent source processes
- Source: This is a Will Kelly composite estimate derived from practitioner observation, not a single published study
- Caveat: Do not present as external research. Frame as: "Practitioner experience across enterprise AI engagements suggests..." or note in speaker notes that this is a field estimate
- Slide usage: Problem framing, not headline stat

## Source credibility hierarchy for executive audiences

Rank sources in this order when choosing what to cite on a slide. Executive audiences, especially CFOs and CIOs, will mentally rank your sources too.

1. **Tier 1 — cite by name**: McKinsey, Gartner, IDC, Forrester, Harvard Business Review, MIT Sloan Management Review
2. **Tier 2 — cite with qualifier**: SHRM, HDI, IBM Institute for Business Value, Deloitte Insights ("Deloitte research suggests...")
3. **Tier 3 — cite with framing**: Industry association research, vendor-sponsored studies ("According to a [Vendor]-commissioned study...")
4. **Tier 4 — frame as practitioner insight**: Will's own field observation, composite estimates, pattern recognition across engagements ("Across enterprise AI engagements, practitioners consistently report...")
5. **Never cite**: Anonymous blog posts, Reddit/forums, undated web content, Wikipedia, AI-generated statistics

## Statistics to avoid — common AI hype claims that get challenged

These appear frequently in AI decks and get picked apart by skeptical CIOs and CFOs. Do not use without a specific, named source:

- "AI will add $X trillion to the global economy" — macro projection, not actionable, always vendor-inflated
- "Companies using AI are X% more productive" — almost always vendor-sponsored
- "AI adoption is accelerating faster than any previous technology" — unfalsifiable, sounds like vendor theater
- "Most companies will have AI deployed by [year]" — prediction, not evidence
- Any percentage cited without a named source and year
