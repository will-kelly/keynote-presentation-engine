# Audience and tone calibration — Keynote presentation engine

Every deck Will delivers goes to a specific decision-maker or stakeholder group. This file maps audience type to the correct register, argument structure, visual density, and risk framing. Get the audience wrong and the deck fails before the first slide is advanced.

---

## Audience profiles

### CIO (Chief Information Officer)

**What they care about**: Operational risk, IT portfolio coherence, vendor accountability, team capability, and staying out of the headline for the wrong reasons.

**What they fear**: Presenting something to the board that gets picked apart. Initiatives that stall in production. Being the person who approved a failed AI pilot.

**How they read a deck**:
- Skim titles first. If the titles don't make claims, they disengage.
- Jump to the recommendation slide before reading anything else.
- Spend most of their attention on the risk and ROI slides.
- Will ask: "Where's this number from?" and "Who owns this after you leave?"

**Tone for CIO decks**:
- Direct, no hedging
- Risk-first framing — lead with what could go wrong, then show the fix
- Peer-level language — you are advising, not selling
- No vendor theater, no buzzwords, no "transformation" without specifics
- Acknowledge what you do not know; CIOs respect intellectual honesty over false confidence

**Slide density**: Medium — 3–5 content blocks per slide. CIOs read fast and want room to think.

**What to avoid**:
- Long bullet lists (signals the presenter hasn't synthesized)
- Charts without axis labels or sources (signals sloppiness)
- Recommendations with no risk acknowledgment (signals naivety)
- Any "in conclusion" or "thank you" as a final slide heading

### CFO (Chief Financial Officer)

**What they care about**: Dollar-denominated costs and benefits, payback period, risk to the balance sheet, compliance exposure, and whether the ask fits in the budget cycle.

**What they fear**: Approving spend that doesn't produce a measurable return. Being surprised by a cost they didn't see coming.

**How they read a deck**:
- Go straight to the numbers. If there are no numbers, the deck is not serious.
- Want to see assumptions, not just outcomes. "Where did that 40% come from?"
- Will ask: "What does this cost all-in?" and "What happens if we do nothing?"

**Tone for CFO decks**:
- Conservative — don't overclaim the upside
- Dollar-denominated throughout — translate every metric to cost or risk
- Show the do-nothing cost, not just the investment cost
- Attribution on every number — CFOs will trace citations
- Short sentences, no jargon unless it's financial jargon they own

**Slide density**: Low — big numbers, brief explanations. CFOs hate visual clutter.

**What to avoid**:
- Productivity claims without a methodology (CFOs will ask how you measured it)
- Vendor-sponsored statistics without attribution disclosure
- ROI projections without stated assumptions
- Slides that mix costs and benefits in the same visual without clear labeling

### CTO (Chief Technology Officer)

**What they care about**: Technical feasibility, architecture coherence, team skill gaps, build-vs-buy decisions, and technical debt accumulation.

**What they fear**: Inheriting a system that breaks at scale or creates long-term maintenance burden.

**How they read a deck**:
- Want to see the technical architecture or approach, not just the outcome
- Will probe assumptions: "What happens when this fails?" "Who maintains it?"
- Comfortable with complexity — don't oversimplify for a CTO audience

**Tone for CTO decks**:
- Technically credible — use precise language, not marketing language
- Show the approach, not just the output
- Acknowledge trade-offs and limitations — CTOs respect honesty about constraints
- Reference Will's practitioner experience explicitly — GDIT, Docker, Anchore context earns credibility here

**Slide density**: Medium-high — CTOs will engage with detail if it's technically relevant.

**What to avoid**:
- Oversimplified architecture diagrams that omit real complexity
- Claims about AI capability that aren't grounded in specific model or tool context
- Process diagrams that don't reflect how systems actually work

### VP of Content / Head of Content Operations

**What they care about**: Team capacity, tool stack coherence, editorial workflow efficiency, governance, and making the case upward for content investment.

**What they fear**: Being the bottleneck. Not having the budget or headcount to fix what they know is broken.

**How they read a deck**:
- Most likely to have already read the problem — they live it
- Want validation of what they already suspect, plus language they can use with their CFO or CTO
- Will engage with methodology detail more than other audience types

**Tone for content ops decks**:
- Peer-to-peer and direct — they know the dysfunction, skip the education
- Arm them with CFO-ready language: dollar costs, risk framing, compliance exposure
- Acknowledge the resource constraints without excusing inaction

**Slide density**: Medium — they can handle detail, but they're also time-poor.

**What to avoid**:
- Talking down to them about content quality basics they already know
- Presenting a framework without a clear path to implementation
- Recommending tools without addressing the governance that makes tools work

### Board of directors / audit committee

**What they care about**: Governance, fiduciary risk, regulatory exposure, and whether management has a credible plan.

**What they fear**: Surprises. Things that should have been disclosed earlier. Management without a clear plan.

**How they read a deck**:
- Board members read executive summaries, not detail slides
- They want: what is the risk, what is the plan, who owns it, when will it be resolved
- One board member will ask the adversarial question — design the deck assuming that person is in the room

**Tone for board decks**:
- Formal register, not conversational
- Risk-quantified — dollar exposure, not "significant risk"
- Timeline-specific — "resolved by Q3" not "in the near term"
- Ownership-clear — a named person or role, not "the team"

**Slide density**: Very low — board slides should be readable in 30 seconds each.

**What to avoid**:
- Technical jargon without plain-English translation
- Slides that require explanation to be understood (if it needs explanation, rewrite it)
- Recommendations without owners and dates
- Anything that looks like the presenter is still figuring it out

### Conference audience (thought leadership keynote)

**What they care about**: Insights they haven't heard before, validation of their own frustrations, practical takeaways they can use, and the speaker's credibility.

**What they fear**: Wasting 45 minutes on a vendor pitch disguised as a keynote.

**How they engage with a deck**:
- Watch the speaker more than the slides — slides should support, not replace the talk
- Want one memorable idea they can take back to their team
- Will disengage if slides are text-heavy or look like a document

**Tone for keynote decks**:
- Will's confrontational, pattern-recognition-forward voice — this is where it lands best
- One strong argument per talk, supported by examples and data
- Practitioner credibility front-and-center — "I've lived this, not just studied it"
- Anti-vendor-theater framing earns credibility with a practitioner audience

**Slide density**: Very low — conference slides should be almost visual-only. Text goes in the speaker notes.

**What to avoid**:
- More than 6 words on any single slide (for visual-only slides)
- Slides that work as standalone documents (they're slides, not handouts)
- Ending with a product pitch — end with a challenge or call to action for the audience

## Tone quick-reference by deck type

| Deck type | Register | Lead with | Risk framing |
|-----------|----------|-----------|--------------|
| Executive briefing | Advisory, peer-level | Recommendation | Explicit, first 3 slides |
| Business case | Conservative, CFO-calibrated | Dollar cost of inaction | ROI with stated assumptions |
| Consulting readout | Direct, practitioner | Scored findings | Ranked by severity |
| Services pitch | Confident, not pushy | Their problem | "Here's what happens if this isn't fixed" |
| Thought leadership keynote | Confrontational, editorial | Counterintuitive claim | Embedded in the argument |
| AI readiness diagnostic | Clinical, evidence-based | Domain scores | Gap-to-risk mapping |
| Documentation debt audit | Risk-framed, CFO-ready | Dollar cost | Severity-ranked |
| Content ops maturity | Practitioner-level | Maturity score | Roadmap with sequenced priorities |

## Red flag language — never use in any deck

These phrases signal vendor theater and destroy credibility with executive audiences:

| Banned phrase | Replace with |
|---------------|-------------|
| "Cutting-edge" | Name the specific technology and its actual capability |
| "Transformative" | State the specific outcome and timeline |
| "Game-changing" | Delete. State what changes and for whom. |
| "Best-in-class" | Cite the benchmark and who set it |
| "Synergy" | Describe the specific integration or efficiency |
| "Leverage" (as verb) | Use, apply, or deploy |
| "Holistic approach" | List the specific components |
| "Seamless" | Describe the integration requirement |
| "AI-powered" without specifics | Name the model, tool, or method |
| "Digital transformation" | Name the process being changed and the outcome |
| "Moving forward" | State the specific next step |
| "In conclusion" | Just conclude — don't announce it |
| "Exciting opportunity" | State the opportunity in measurable terms |
| "Fractional" (Will self-describing) | Independent consulting or consulting engagement |
